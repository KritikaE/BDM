  <!-- HR as a Function -->
  <div class="section">
    <div class="section-head"><span class="section-icon">🏢</span><h3>HR as a Function</h3></div>
    <div class="section-body">
      <div class="def-box"><strong>Definition:</strong> HR (Human Resources) is the organizational function responsible for managing the full employee lifecycle — from attracting talent to developing, engaging, and retaining people.</div>
      <p><strong>Core HR Sub-Functions:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Talent Acquisition</strong> – Recruitment, sourcing, onboarding</li>
        <li><strong>Learning & Development</strong> – Training, upskilling, L&D programs</li>
        <li><strong>Compensation & Benefits</strong> – Payroll, incentives, perks</li>
        <li><strong>Performance Management</strong> – Appraisals, KPIs, feedback cycles</li>
        <li><strong>Employee Relations</strong> – Grievances, culture, compliance</li>
        <li><strong>HR Analytics</strong> – Data-driven decision making in people operations</li>
      </ul>
      <div class="eg-box"><strong>Example:</strong> At Infosys, HR function manages 300,000+ employees through specialized sub-units — Talent Supply Chain handles recruitment while a separate team handles performance analytics.</div>
    </div>
  </div>

  <!-- Internal Sourcing & Recruitment -->
  <div class="section">
    <div class="section-head"><span class="section-icon">🔍</span><h3>Recruitment Process & Sourcing</h3></div>
    <div class="section-body">
      <div class="def-box"><strong>Internal Sourcing:</strong> Filling open positions from the existing employee pool through promotions, lateral transfers, or internal job postings (IJPs).</div>
      <div class="def-box"><strong>External Sourcing:</strong> Attracting candidates from outside the organization via job portals, referrals, campuses, agencies, etc.</div>

      <p><strong>Recruitment Process (Step by Step):</strong></p>
      <ol>
        <li><strong>Requisition/Indent</strong> – Formal hiring trigger raised by a department manager</li>
        <li><strong>Job Description (JD)</strong> – Document outlining role, responsibilities, skills, qualifications</li>
        <li><strong>Sourcing</strong> – Identifying candidates through channels</li>
        <li><strong>Screening</strong> – Shortlisting based on criteria</li>
        <li><strong>Selection</strong> – Interviews, assessments, scoring</li>
        <li><strong>Offer & Onboarding</strong> – Extending offer and integrating new hire</li>
      </ol>

      <div class="def-box"><strong>Requisition / Indent:</strong> The formal document/process that triggers the hiring cycle. It includes role title, grade, budget approval, number of positions, and justification. <em>Without an approved requisition, no hiring begins.</em></div>

      <div class="def-box"><strong>Job Description (JD):</strong> A structured document containing: Job Title, Role Summary, Key Responsibilities, Required Skills (Technical + Soft), Qualifications, Reporting structure, Location/Compensation band.</div>

      <div class="insight-box"><strong>Insight:</strong> A well-written JD is the foundation of a good hire — a vague JD leads to mismatched candidates and wasted time in screening.</div>

      <p><strong>Sourcing Channels & Effectiveness:</strong></p>
      <table>
        <tr><th>Channel</th><th>Best For</th><th>Effectiveness</th></tr>
        <tr><td>Employee Referrals</td><td>Culture-fit, Trust</td><td>⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐ Highest retention</td></tr>
        <tr><td>LinkedIn / Naukri</td><td>Mid-senior roles</td><td>⭐⭐⭐⭐</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Campus Recruitment</td><td>Fresher hiring</td><td>⭐⭐⭐⭐</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Staffing Agencies</td><td>Contract/Niche roles</td><td>⭐⭐⭐</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Job Boards (Indeed)</td><td>Volume hiring</td><td>⭐⭐⭐</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Internal Job Postings</td><td>Succession planning</td><td>⭐⭐⭐⭐ Low cost</td></tr>
      </table>

      <div class="insight-box"><strong>Channel Effectiveness:</strong> Measured by Cost-per-hire, Time-to-fill, Quality-of-hire, and Offer acceptance rate. Referrals typically have the lowest cost and highest quality.</div>

      <p><strong>Gender Diversity in Recruitment:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Ensuring balanced gender representation across hiring pipelines</li>
        <li>Using blind resume screening to reduce unconscious bias</li>
        <li>Setting diversity targets (e.g., 30% women in tech hires)</li>
        <li>Tracking diversity metrics: gender ratio by department, offer acceptance by gender</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </div>

  <!-- Selection & Scoring -->
  <div class="section">
    <div class="section-head"><span class="section-icon">🎯</span><h3>Selection Criteria, Composite Scoring & Candidate Ranking</h3></div>
    <div class="section-body">
      <div class="def-box"><strong>Selection Criteria:</strong> Objective benchmarks used to evaluate candidates — e.g., years of experience, test score, communication rating, technical skills rating.</div>

      <p><strong>Composite Scoring:</strong> Combining multiple selection parameters into a single score using weighted averages.</p>
      <div class="formula-box">
        Composite Score = (W₁ × Score₁) + (W₂ × Score₂) + ... + (Wₙ × Scoreₙ)<br>
        where W₁ + W₂ + ... + Wₙ = 1 (weights sum to 1)
      </div>
      <div class="eg-box"><strong>Example:</strong> Technical Test (40%) + Interview Score (35%) + Communication (25%) → Composite Score = 0.4×85 + 0.35×78 + 0.25×80 = 34+27.3+20 = <strong>81.3</strong></div>

      <p><strong>Max-Normalization Formula:</strong> Used to scale variables to a 0–1 range before comparing candidates with different scales.</p>
      <div class="formula-box">
        Normalized Score = (Value − Min) / (Max − Min)
      </div>
      <div class="eg-box"><strong>Example:</strong> Test scores range 40–95. Candidate scores 75. Normalized = (75−40)/(95−40) = 35/55 = <strong>0.636</strong></div>
      <div class="insight-box"><strong>Why Normalize?</strong> Without normalization, a variable with a large scale (e.g., salary expectation in lakhs) would dominate composite scores unfairly over a 1–10 rating scale.</div>

      <p><strong>Weighted Scoring for "Lower-is-Better" Metrics:</strong></p>
      <p>Some metrics are better when lower (e.g., Notice Period in days, Salary Expectation). To include these in composite scoring:</p>
      <div class="formula-box">
        Inverted Normalized Score = 1 − [(Value − Min) / (Max − Min)]
      </div>
      <div class="eg-box"><strong>Example:</strong> Notice period of 30 days when range is 0–90 days. Inverted = 1 − (30/90) = 1 − 0.33 = <strong>0.67</strong> (shorter notice = higher score = better)</div>

      <p><strong>Candidate Ranking:</strong> After composite scoring, candidates are ranked in descending order. Top-N candidates proceed to the next round. Ties are broken by a pre-defined tiebreaker (e.g., technical score).</p>
    </div>
  </div>

  <!-- HR Analytics -->
  <div class="section">
    <div class="section-head"><span class="section-icon">📊</span><h3>HR Analytics, Skills Availability & Manpower Forecasting</h3></div>
    <div class="section-body">
      <div class="def-box"><strong>HR Analytics:</strong> The use of data, statistics, and predictive models to make evidence-based HR decisions — covering recruitment, retention, performance, and workforce planning.</div>

      <p><strong>Types of HR Analytics:</strong></p>
      <table>
        <tr><th>Type</th><th>Description</th><th>Example</th></tr>
        <tr><td>Descriptive</td><td>What happened?</td><td>Monthly attrition rate last year</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Diagnostic</td><td>Why did it happen?</td><td>Why did attrition spike in Q3?</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Predictive</td><td>What will happen?</td><td>Which employees may leave in 6 months?</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Prescriptive</td><td>What should we do?</td><td>Recommend retention bonuses for at-risk employees</td></tr>
      </table>

      <div class="def-box"><strong>Skills Availability:</strong> Assessment of whether the current workforce has the competencies required for present and future roles. Involves skills mapping and gap analysis.</div>

      <div class="def-box"><strong>Manpower Forecasting:</strong> Predicting the number and type of employees needed at a future point in time, based on business growth plans, attrition rates, and project pipeline.</div>

      <p><strong>Matching Supply with Demand:</strong></p>
      <div class="formula-box">
        Future Supply = Current Headcount − Expected Attrition + Expected Hires/Promotions<br>
        Gap = Forecasted Demand − Forecasted Supply
      </div>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Surplus:</strong> More employees than needed → redeployment or freeze on hiring</li>
        <li><strong>Deficit:</strong> Fewer employees than needed → trigger recruitment or upskilling</li>
      </ul>
      <div class="eg-box"><strong>Example:</strong> IT company needs 500 Java developers in Q3. Current pool: 420. Expected attrition: 40. Supply = 420−40 = 380. Gap = 500−380 = <strong>120 developers to hire.</strong></div>

      <p><strong>Attrition Rates:</strong></p>
      <div class="formula-box">
        Attrition Rate = (No. of Employees who Left / Average Headcount) × 100
      </div>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Industry Benchmarks (India):</strong> IT/Tech: 18–25% | BPO/ITES: 30–40% | BFSI: 15–20% | Manufacturing: 8–12%</li>
        <li><strong>Impact of High Attrition:</strong> Increased recruitment costs, knowledge loss, low morale, project delays</li>
        <li><strong>Voluntary vs Involuntary:</strong> Voluntary (employee-initiated) is more concerning from a retention standpoint</li>
      </ul>

      <div class="def-box"><strong>The Bench:</strong> Employees who are trained and available but not currently assigned to any project or billing. Also called "unallocated resources." Managing the bench well is critical — bench time = cost with no revenue.</div>
      <div class="insight-box"><strong>Bench Management Strategy:</strong> Keep bench duration short (ideally &lt;30 days), use bench time for upskilling, and track bench utilization rates. High bench = poor manpower planning.</div>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="section">
    <div class="section-head"><span class="section-icon">💳</span><h3>Fintech Basics & Payments Industry</h3></div>
    <div class="section-body">
      <div class="def-box"><strong>Fintech (Financial Technology):</strong> Companies or technologies that use software and innovation to deliver financial services — payments, lending, insurance, investment — more efficiently than traditional banks.</div>
      <p><strong>Key Fintech Verticals:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Payments</strong> – UPI, Wallets, Cards (PhonePe, GPay, Paytm)</li>
        <li><strong>Lending</strong> – Digital loans, BNPL, P2P (KreditBee, LazyPay)</li>
        <li><strong>Wealth Tech</strong> – Robo-advisors, mutual funds (Zerodha, Groww)</li>
        <li><strong>InsurTech</strong> – Digital insurance (PolicyBazaar, Acko)</li>
        <li><strong>RegTech</strong> – Compliance automation</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Money Flow in Payments:</strong></p>
      <div class="formula-box">
        Customer → Merchant → Payment Gateway → Acquiring Bank → Card Network → Issuing Bank
      </div>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Payment Gateway:</strong> Technology that captures, encrypts, and transmits payment data (Razorpay, Stripe)</li>
        <li><strong>Acquiring Bank:</strong> The merchant's bank that receives the payment</li>
        <li><strong>Issuing Bank:</strong> The customer's bank that approves/declines the transaction</li>
        <li><strong>MDR (Merchant Discount Rate):</strong> Fee charged to merchant on every transaction (~1.5–2% for cards)</li>
      </ul>
      <div class="eg-box"><strong>Example:</strong> You pay ₹1000 on Zomato via Visa card. The money flows: You → Razorpay (gateway) → HDFC (acquirer) → Visa Network → Your bank (SBI). Zomato receives ~₹980 after MDR.</div>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="section">
    <div class="section-head"><span class="section-icon">📱</span><h3>UPI Ecosystem</h3></div>
    <div class="section-body">
      <div class="def-box"><strong>UPI (Unified Payments Interface):</strong> A real-time payment system developed by NPCI (National Payments Corporation of India) that enables instant interbank money transfers using a Virtual Payment Address (VPA/UPI ID).</div>
      <p><strong>Key Stakeholders:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>NPCI</strong> – Regulator and infrastructure owner</li>
        <li><strong>PSP (Payment Service Provider)</strong> – Apps like PhonePe, GPay built on UPI rails</li>
        <li><strong>Banks</strong> – Store the actual money and process transactions</li>
        <li><strong>Merchant</strong> – Receiver of payment via UPI QR or ID</li>
      </ul>
      <table>
        <tr><th>Feature</th><th>Details</th></tr>
        <tr><td>Transaction Limit</td><td>₹1 lakh per transaction (₹2L for select categories)</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Available 24/7</td><td>Yes, including holidays</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Settlement</td><td>Real-time (T+0)</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Cost to User</td><td>Free (zero MDR on P2P)</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Volume (2024)</td><td>~10 billion transactions/month</td></tr>
      </table>
      <div class="insight-box"><strong>UPI 2.0 Features:</strong> Overdraft account linkage, signed intent, recurring payments, invoice in the inbox. India's UPI is now accepted in Singapore, UAE, France, and other countries.</div>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="section">
    <div class="section-head"><span class="section-icon">🛍️</span><h3>BNPL – Buy Now, Pay Later</h3></div>
    <div class="section-body">
      <div class="def-box"><strong>BNPL:</strong> A short-term financing product that allows consumers to make purchases immediately and pay in installments (usually 3–12 EMIs), often interest-free if paid on time.</div>
      <p><strong>How BNPL Works:</strong></p>
      <ol>
        <li>Customer selects BNPL at checkout</li>
        <li>BNPL provider (e.g., LazyPay, Simpl) pays merchant in full</li>
        <li>Customer repays provider in installments</li>
        <li>Provider charges merchant a fee; charges customer late fees if repayment delayed</li>
      </ol>
      <div class="two-col">
        <div>
          <p><strong>Benefits:</strong></p>
          <ul>
            <li>No credit card needed</li>
            <li>Instant approval</li>
            <li>Drives conversion for merchants</li>
            <li>Financial inclusion for thin-file customers</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
        <div>
          <p><strong>Risks:</strong></p>
          <ul>
            <li>Over-indebtedness for users</li>
            <li>High default risk</li>
            <li>Regulatory scrutiny increasing</li>
            <li>Impulsive spending behavior</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </div>
      <div class="eg-box"><strong>PayBuddy Case Study:</strong> A fictional fintech offering BNPL to young consumers. Key lesson: Segment customers by repayment behavior, use nudges to trigger timely repayments, monitor cohort-level default rates monthly, and adjust credit limits dynamically based on behavioral signals.</div>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="section">
    <div class="section-head"><span class="section-icon">🧠</span><h3>Behavioral Economics & Nudge Theory</h3></div>
    <div class="section-body">
      <div class="def-box"><strong>Behavioral Economics:</strong> Study of how psychological biases and heuristics influence financial and economic decisions — people are not always rational actors.</div>
      <div class="def-box"><strong>Nudge Economics (Thaler & Sunstein):</strong> Designing choice environments ("choice architecture") to guide people toward better decisions without restricting freedom. A nudge is any small design change that predictably alters behavior.</div>

      <p><strong>Key Behavioral Biases:</strong></p>
      <table>
        <tr><th>Bias</th><th>Description</th><th>Fintech Application</th></tr>
        <tr><td>Default Bias</td><td>People stick with defaults</td><td>BNPL pre-selected as payment option</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Loss Aversion</td><td>Losses feel 2x worse than equivalent gains</td><td>"You'll lose your reward if you don't pay by Friday"</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Present Bias</td><td>Prefer immediate rewards over future ones</td><td>Users delay repayments → use countdown timers</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Social Proof</td><td>Follow what others do</td><td>"95% of users like you pay on time"</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Framing Effect</td><td>How info is presented changes decisions</td><td>"Save ₹200" vs "Avoid losing ₹200"</td></tr>
      </table>

      <p><strong>Nudge Factors in Fintech (PayBuddy context):</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>SMS/notification reminders 3 days before due date</li>
        <li>Progress bars showing "EMI 2 of 3 paid"</li>
        <li>Reward points for on-time payment</li>
        <li>Visual cues (red for overdue, green for paid)</li>
        <li>Auto-pay enrollment (default to opt-in)</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Customer Segmentation for Credit Marketing:</strong></p>
      <div class="def-box"><strong>Customer Segmentation:</strong> Dividing a customer base into groups with similar characteristics for targeted marketing, product design, or risk management.</div>
      <p><strong>Common Segmentation Variables (Credit Datasets):</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Demographics:</strong> Age, income, location, profession</li>
        <li><strong>Behavioral:</strong> Transaction frequency, average ticket size, repayment history</li>
        <li><strong>Credit Profile:</strong> Credit score, existing debt, credit utilization ratio</li>
        <li><strong>Engagement:</strong> App opens, notification clicks, feature usage</li>
      </ul>
      <div class="eg-box"><strong>Segment Analysis Example:</strong> Segment A – Young salaried, low credit score, high app engagement → offer small BNPL limits with nudges. Segment B – Middle-aged, high income, good credit → offer higher limits, credit cards, investment products.</div>
    </div>
  </div>

    <span class="week-badge">WEEK 11</span>

  <div class="section">
    <div class="section-head"><span class="section-icon">🧪</span><h3>A/B Testing</h3></div>
    <div class="section-body">
      <div class="def-box"><strong>A/B Testing:</strong> A controlled experiment where two versions of a product/feature (A = control, B = test) are shown to different user groups simultaneously to determine which performs better on a defined metric.</div>

      <p><strong>Key Concepts:</strong></p>
      <table>
        <tr><th>Term</th><th>Definition</th></tr>
        <tr><td>Control Group (A)</td><td>Users who see the original/existing version. The baseline.</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Test Group (B)</td><td>Users who see the new/modified version being evaluated.</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Conversion Rate</td><td>% of users who complete a desired action (purchase, sign-up, repayment)</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Retention Rate</td><td>% of users who return to the app/product after a defined period</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Statistical Significance</td><td>Confidence that observed difference is not due to random chance (typically p &lt; 0.05)</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Sample Size</td><td>Minimum users needed per group to detect a meaningful effect</td></tr>
      </table>

      <div class="formula-box">
        Conversion Rate = (Users who Converted / Total Users in Group) × 100<br><br>
        Lift = (Conversion Rate B − Conversion Rate A) / Conversion Rate A × 100
      </div>

      <p><strong>A/B Testing Process:</strong></p>
      <ol>
        <li><strong>Hypothesis</strong> – "Adding a progress bar will increase repayment rate"</li>
        <li><strong>Design</strong> – Split users randomly 50/50 into control and test</li>
        <li><strong>Run</strong> – Run experiment for sufficient duration (usually 2–4 weeks)</li>
        <li><strong>Measure</strong> – Compare conversion/retention rates between groups</li>
        <li><strong>Decide</strong> – If statistically significant improvement → ship the feature</li>
      </ol>

      <div class="eg-box"><strong>Example:</strong> PayBuddy tests a new "pay in 1-click" button. Control group: 12% repayment rate. Test group: 16% repayment rate. Lift = (16−12)/12 × 100 = 33% improvement → strong signal to ship.</div>

      <p><strong>Experiment Analysis:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Segment by cohort:</strong> Check if results differ across age groups, locations</li>
        <li><strong>Watch for novelty effect:</strong> Users may engage just because something is new</li>
        <li><strong>Avoid peeking:</strong> Don't stop the test as soon as you see a positive result</li>
        <li><strong>Multi-variate testing:</strong> Test multiple changes simultaneously (more complex)</li>
      </ul>

      <div class="insight-box"><strong>Product Optimization:</strong> A/B testing is the core engine of product improvement. Top tech companies (Google, Uber, Swiggy) run thousands of A/B tests per year to optimize UX, conversion funnels, pricing, and engagement.</div>
    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="section">
    <div class="section-head"><span class="section-icon">⚠️</span><h3>Credit Risk Analysis & Loan Default</h3></div>
    <div class="section-body">
      <div class="def-box"><strong>Credit Risk:</strong> The probability that a borrower will fail to repay a loan as per agreed terms. Managing credit risk is central to any lending business.</div>
      <div class="def-box"><strong>Loan Default:</strong> When a borrower fails to make scheduled payments. Formally defined as 90+ days past due (DPD) in most institutions.</div>

      <p><strong>Key Credit Risk Indicators:</strong></p>
      <table>
        <tr><th>Indicator</th><th>What it Signals</th></tr>
        <tr><td>Credit Score (CIBIL)</td><td>Past repayment behavior (300–900; &gt;750 is good)</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Debt-to-Income (DTI) Ratio</td><td>Total monthly debt / Monthly income. High DTI = higher risk</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Credit Utilization</td><td>% of available credit used. &gt;80% = stressed borrower</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Delinquency History</td><td>Number of past-due events in last 12/24 months</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Employment Stability</td><td>Tenure at current job; unemployed = high risk</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Loan Purpose</td><td>Business loans carry higher risk than secured home loans</td></tr>
      </table>

      <div class="formula-box">
        DTI Ratio = Total Monthly Debt Payments / Gross Monthly Income<br>
        (e.g., ₹15,000 debt / ₹50,000 income = 0.30 or 30% DTI)
      </div>

      <p><strong>Credit Datasets – Key Variables:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Age, Gender, Income, Employment Type</li>
        <li>Loan Amount, Loan Tenure, Interest Rate</li>
        <li>CIBIL Score, Number of existing loans</li>
        <li>Payment history (on-time / late / default)</li>
        <li>Days Past Due (DPD), Number of EMIs missed</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Monthly Trend Analysis:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li>Track default rate month-on-month by cohort (users who took loans in same month)</li>
        <li>Identify if defaults are rising → may indicate bad underwriting or macroeconomic stress</li>
        <li>Plot <strong>Vintage Analysis</strong>: % of each loan cohort that defaulted at 1/3/6/12 months</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Financial Behavior Interpretation:</strong></p>
      <ul>
        <li><strong>Revolving credit high utilization</strong> → spending beyond means, higher default risk</li>
        <li><strong>Multiple new loan inquiries</strong> → may be credit-hungry / distressed</li>
        <li><strong>Part-payment behavior</strong> → intent to repay but cash-flow constrained → lower risk</li>
        <li><strong>Transaction frequency drop</strong> → customer churning or financial stress</li>
      </ul>

      <div class="eg-box"><strong>Case Study:</strong> A BNPL firm finds that users who made 2+ late payments in Month 1 have a 4× higher default rate by Month 6. They use this as an early warning flag to restrict credit limits proactively.</div>
    </div>
  </div>

        <li>Example: Order-to-Cash workflow: Order received → Fulfillment → Delivery → Invoice → Payment</li>
        <li><strong>Cross-functional coordination:</strong> Most workflows require multiple departments (Sales quotes a price, Finance approves, Ops delivers, Customer Success follows up)</li>
        <li><strong>SOPs (Standard Operating Procedures):</strong> Documented instructions for repeatable tasks</li>
      </ul>

      <div class="insight-box"><strong>Insight:</strong> Operational bottlenecks usually occur at handoff points between departments. Good companies invest in process documentation and integration tools (ERP, CRM) to reduce friction.</div>

    <div class="section-head"><span class="section-icon">♟️</span><h3>Strategic vs Operational Management</h3></div>
    <div class="section-body">
      <table>
        <tr><th>Dimension</th><th>Strategic Management</th><th>Operational Management</th></tr>
        <tr><td>Time Horizon</td><td>3–5 years (long-term)</td><td>Daily, weekly, monthly</td></tr>
        <tr><td>Focus</td><td>Where to compete, what to build</td><td>How to execute, deliver, optimize</td></tr>

        <tr><td>Risk</td><td>High (big bets)</td><td>Lower (routine execution)</td></tr>
      </table>
      <div class="def-box"><strong>Decision-Making Systems:</strong> Structured frameworks used by businesses to make choices — includes data dashboards, approval workflows, committee reviews, and AI recommendations.</div>
    </div>

  <div class="section">
    <div class="section-head"><span class="section-icon">📋</span><h3>Spreadsheet-Based Data Processing</h3></div>
    <div class="section-body">
      <div class="def-box"><strong>Business Data Organization:</strong> Structuring raw data in a systematic way (tables, databases, spreadsheets) so it can be easily analyzed, filtered, and used for decisions.</div>

      <p><strong>Data Cleaning:</strong> The process of detecting and correcting errors in a dataset before analysis.</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Remove duplicates</li>
        <li>Handle missing values (fill with average, median, or remove row)</li>
        <li>Standardize formats (dates: DD/MM/YYYY, names: Title Case)</li>
        <li>Remove outliers that are clearly errors (age = 999)</li>
        <li>Validate data types (numeric columns shouldn't have text)</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Sorting:</strong> Arranging data in ascending or descending order based on a column.</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Single-column sort: Sort employees by salary descending</li>
        <li>Multi-level sort: Sort by department (A→Z), then by salary within each department (high→low)</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Filtering:</strong> Displaying only rows that meet a specified condition.</p>
      <ul>
        <li>Filter employees in "Sales" department with salary &gt; ₹8L</li>
        <li>Filter loan records where DPD &gt; 30</li>
        <li>Filter candidates who scored &gt; 75 in technical test</li>
      </ul>

      <p><strong>Key Spreadsheet Functions for Business:</strong></p>
      <table>
        <tr><th>Function</th><th>Use Case</th></tr>
        <tr><td>VLOOKUP / XLOOKUP</td><td>Match data across two tables (e.g., joining employee ID to salary data)</td></tr>
        <tr><td>SUMIF / COUNTIF</td><td>Conditional aggregation (total sales for Region=North)</td></tr>
        <tr><td>PIVOT TABLE</td><td>Summarize large datasets by category (sales by month, by region)</td></tr>
        <tr><td>IF / IFS</td><td>Conditional logic (if score &gt;80 then "Shortlisted" else "Rejected")</td></tr>
        <tr><td>TEXT / DATE functions</td><td>Clean and format data</td></tr>
      </table>

      <div class="eg-box"><strong>Business Case:</strong> HR team has a raw attendance dataset of 5000 employees. They clean it (remove blanks, fix dates), filter for employees with &lt;80% attendance, sort by department, and use COUNTIF to find how many are in each department — final output feeds into a performance review report.</div>

      <div class="insight-box"><strong>Exam Tip:</strong> Data cleaning → Sorting → Filtering → Aggregation (SUMIF/Pivot) → Visualization is the standard workflow for any business data analysis task. Memorize this pipeline.</div>
    </div>
